Astronomy

Will we ever explore the stars

Credit: Shuttershock

By: Dante Lentsoe

Star Wars, Star Trek, Carl Sagan, Elon Musk, and Jeff Bezos. What do all those movie franchises and people have in common? Well, space exploration. For years we have wondered if other worlds home life, and truth be told it is one of the leading reasons why Sci-Fi movies were a hit and success in the 1980's.

What does it mean for us, in our reality? Well, provided that the occasional cosmic radiation, asteroid debris, freezing temperatures of the open cosmos, distant planets, limitations in our spacecrafts, importance of gravity, the need of oxygen and food do not bother you, well you are probably good to go. But realistically speaking, it is not a good investment. At least traveling in your physical biological form.

The cosmic speed limit, commonly known as speed of light, is resounding 299 792 458 m/s (metres per second) or 299 792.458 km/s (kilometres per second). Well to put that into perspective, at that speed it is the equivalent of moving from London (England) to New York (United States) roughly 54 times in a second. Now that is speed. Even at this speed, interstellar travel presents problems, due to vast distances of the cosmos. Our nearest neighbouring star system, Alpha Centauri, is about 4 light years away, meaning for the cosmic speed limit to reach the nearest star system, takes it 4 years. 4 YEARS!!! Now that is far. For human being to travel in space ships, nevermind our fastest spacecraft (Parker Solar Probe) having a shameful 192.227 km/s, and this was achieved using gravity assit from planets in our solar system. Clearly, the practicallity of a Star Wars type-ship seems unreasonable, so are we doomed to stay in our solar system, well no. There is an alternative.

It turns out computer science, neuro science and science fiction have to blur lines. Researchers have proposed Whole Brain Emulation (WBE), which is sampling a brain, then modeling it from scanning then porting the data from that particular brain into new hardware whilst retaining the same information. Simply put, uploading your consciousness into a digital form, that can be transfered to any where, at any point, you existing in a digital form.

For the purpose of staying on topic, I won't go into finer details of how brain tissue is sampled, modelled and how the data is stored and processed. At this stage the technology is still far off, to even model less then 10% of rats brain presents a challenge. However, should this technology come to be feasible in the distant future, we could have our consciousness transfered from earth to the moon in seconds, occupy an avatar (most likely a synthetic body) move around the moon, transfer your consciousness again around the solar system, at your leisure. Now that sounds like SCI-FI, but surprizngly it is all within reason and complies with our understanding of physics.

To respond to the question posed by the article's title, yes we could travel to the stars, but not in spacecrafts, and armored suits. The most mind-binding prospect of traveling via statelite transmission, is far more reasonable and realistic, then hoping on ship, with a custom seat label. We are all captains, and leaders of this voyge, as Carl Sagan stated "If we do not live to destroy ourselves, we will travel to the stars".
